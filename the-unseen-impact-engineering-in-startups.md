As someone who balances life as a software engineer at my day job while moonlighting as the CTO of a budding startup, I've gained a unique perspective on the challenges of engineering leadership. While I haven't led massive engineering organisations or built systems serving millions, I've observed an interesting pattern both in my own experiences and in conversations with peers: the critical work of engineering often remains largely unseen, despite being fundamental to a startup's success.

## Straddling Two Worlds

There's something revealing about inhabiting both roles—the individual contributor during office hours and the technical leader during nights and weekends. In my day job, I see how engineering leadership decisions cascade down to affect every aspect of my work. In my startup, I'm the one making those decisions, often with limited resources, incomplete information, and shifting priorities.

This dual perspective has shown me just how essential—and often invisible—engineering work truly is.

## Why I Prioritise Recognising Engineers

Having experienced the often unseen nature of technical work firsthand, I've made it a point to recognise and celebrate the engineering achievements in our startup. I understand the hours of focused effort and problem-solving it takes to implement complex solutions that others might never fully comprehend.

A recent example brings this into sharp focus: A new employee in our startup single-handedly migrated our entire infrastructure from Railway to AWS. This substantial effort took weeks of diligent work, navigating complex systems, and solving numerous unexpected problems. Our product team likely won't even realise anything has changed—the applications still run, users still log in, data still flows.

But for our development team, the transformation has been revolutionary:
- We now have fully integrated CI/CD pipelines
- Our testing is fully automated
- We've implemented GitHub tagged builds for better version control
- We've added multiple environments, including a dedicated Staging environment alongside Production
- Our security posture has dramatically improved by leveraging AWS's specialised services

None of this shows up in user-facing features. No one outside engineering will create slides about it for the next investor pitch. But this invisible work has fundamentally strengthened our foundation, reduced our technical risk, and will accelerate our development for months to come.

I made sure to not only thank this engineer privately but to highlight their achievement to the entire company. Because I know that without explicit recognition, this kind of critical work often remains invisible—and what remains invisible is often undervalued.

## The Infrastructure Behind the Spotlight

In the startup ecosystem I've been part of, I've noticed how the spotlight typically falls on:
- The idea and the pitch
- The fundraising milestones 
- The marketing and growth metrics
- The sales wins and customer acquisitions

Meanwhile, the engineering teams and their leaders—the people actually transforming these promises into reality—often work behind the scenes. They build the infrastructure that supports every aspect of the company, yet their contributions are typically only noticed when something breaks.

When a startup succeeds, the narrative doesn't always acknowledge that without solid engineering:
- The product wouldn't function reliably
- The infrastructure wouldn't handle growth
- Technical debt would impede progress
- Security issues would undermine user trust

## The Balancing Act

What strikes me most about my role as a startup CTO is the balance required:

- Building systems with specifications that evolve frequently
- Delivering reliable products with resource constraints
- Finding technical talent willing to join an early-stage venture
- Planning for future scale while meeting current needs
- Maintaining momentum through strategic shifts

## The Quiet Contributions

In my experience as a startup CTO, I've noticed that engineering contributions often remain under the radar. Some common scenarios:

- **The After-Hours Fix**: You spend an evening solving a critical issue before an important demo. The following day's focus naturally shifts to the successful presentation rather than the behind-the-scenes work that made it possible.

- **The Preventative Work**: You invest time refactoring problematic code that was causing subtle bugs. The main indicator of success is that things simply "stop breaking"—a negative outcome that's harder to celebrate than positive, visible features.

- **The Technical Investments**: You make strategic technical decisions to meet immediate needs while setting up for future growth. These choices rarely receive the same attention as customer-facing features, despite their importance.

- **The Foundation Building**: You advocate for investing in development tooling that will improve efficiency. The long-term benefits might not be immediately apparent to non-technical team members.

## Why This Matters for Success

Even as a relatively new CTO of a small startup, I can see that engineering excellence is a critical factor in our potential success:

1. **Ideas Need Implementation**: Great ideas require skilled execution. The ability to build reliable, efficient, and scalable solutions separates successful startups from struggling ones.

2. **Technical Guidance**: Effective engineering leadership provides crucial perspective on what's feasible within constraints—helping the company set realistic expectations.

3. **Foundational Decisions**: Early technical choices have far-reaching impacts. The right architecture and tech stack create a foundation for future success.

4. **Sustainable Growth**: How engineering teams manage technical debt determines whether development accelerates or slows over time—often a key factor in a startup's trajectory.

## Learning Through Experience

I won't pretend to have all the answers, but even in my limited time as a startup CTO, I've gained some valuable insights:

- The importance of translating technical concepts clearly to non-technical stakeholders
- The need to advocate for essential engineering priorities
- The art of balancing immediate deliverables with long-term sustainability
- The value of building a team that understands both technical excellence and business needs

## Creating Visibility

Based on my observations and experiences, here's my approach to increasing the visibility of engineering contributions:

1. **Technology as core strategy**: Especially in tech startups, the technology isn't just supporting the product—it IS the product. Treat your engineering as central to your business strategy.

2. **Highlight the invisible work**: Make a point of recognising the systems that "just work" because of good engineering.

3. **Include technical voices in decisions**: Ensure technical leaders are involved in major company decisions from the beginning, not just brought in to implement them afterward.

4. **Value technical insights**: When engineering raises concerns about approaches or timelines, they're typically identifying important considerations.

5. **Celebrate engineering milestones**: Make technical achievements as visible and celebrated as sales and marketing wins.

## A Personal Reflection

Though I'm early in my journey as a startup CTO, balancing this role with my day job as a software engineer has given me a new appreciation for the behind-the-scenes nature of engineering work. The technical decisions made today, often during evening hours after a regular workday, shape a startup's ability to execute and scale.

While good work is recognised at times, some of the most impactful engineering contributions remain largely invisible to most of the organisation. The most meaningful feedback I've received wasn't after a major feature launch, but when a co-founder acknowledged a particularly challenging technical problem I'd solved with, "I don't understand exactly what you did, but I can see the difference it's made."

To other engineering leaders in early-stage startups: your work creates the foundation that makes everything else possible. The systems you build and the technical decisions you make often determine whether a startup thrives or struggles.

Without the right engineering foundation, even the most brilliant business ideas remain just aspirations rather than reality.
